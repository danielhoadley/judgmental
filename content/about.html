About Judgmental.org.uk

What is "open justice"?

But is our justice open enough? We think we need to do better.

It is a simple truth that to be able to obey the law, you have to be able to find out what it is. That is, the law, all of the law, must be publicly accessible.

In the UK we have a "common law" legal system, which means that in addition to the law as laid down by Parliament some court decisions actually become part of our law, since we want later decisions to be consistent with previous ones.

Even where they don't directly make a ruling on an issue, decisions of more senior courts are often very helpful in clarifying the law. Judges often refer to the persuasiveness of points of discussion from past judgments, so researching previous judgments which touch upon controversial areas of law can be helpful in predicting how a court might deal with those issues in future.

This means that access to courts' judgments is essential for a full understanding of our laws.

In the past, to make something "public knowledge", it was sufficient to have the town crier make an announcement. Later, in order for information to be publicly accesible it became expected that it should be available in a local library or published in widely-circulated national newspapers, or announced on television. But times have moved on again.

Today, public information cannot reasonably be called "accessible" unless it is freely available online.

While some court judgments are available via BAILII, and HMCS (the Courts Service) publishes some listings, much is still digitally inaccessible, and what is there is difficult to navigate.

What is Judgmental.org.uk?

The Judgmental.org.uk project aims to make all United Kingdom law as accessible and usable as possible. We focus primarily on case law (previous judgments), as this is the area most in need of improvement.

By "accessible", we mean:

  • Present—as many judgments should be available online as possible.
  • Searchable—smart, powerful search is essential for such a large body of documents.
  • Manageable—facilities like tagging and grouping also help filter information.
  • Meaningful—(a.k.a. semantic); specifically marking things like judges' names, times and places makes documents more useful than plain text.

We still have a long way to go in this mission, and you can help!

Please note

Judgmental.org.uk is not finished, and it is not authoritative.

While some judgment texts are drafts obtained directly from judges, it's possible that the judgment was delivered differently when read out in court, so the official record of the judgment would be different.

Further, Judgmental.org.uk is a community effort in its early stages, and the way we manage the judgment documents may change over time. Particularly, documents may be tagged, reformatted or otherwise altered to achieve the accessibility goals mentioned above.

This means that, while we will do our utmost to make sure the information on the site is correct and helpful, you must not use Judgmental.org.uk as a source of legal advice. If you have a legal problem, you should talk to a solicitor, or find out about free legal advice in your area as might be offered by the Citizens' Advice Bureau, the Free Representation Unit, or your regional legal support trust. Your local university law department may also have a legal advice clinic run by students.

Where does the information come from?

In the Summer of 2011, one of our collaborators got a CD in the post containing thousands of judgments. We have no idea who sent it. For that reason, it does not get updated. It seems current up until the middle of 2009.

Why not just use the BAILII website?

BAILII's database has quite a few limitations. For example, their server settings prevent the site from being indexed by search engines, which is how most people find information online. In addition, their own site search facility isn't as good as it could be, and there is no facility for grouping judgments on a topic by tag or cross-referencing.

Judgemental.org.uk on the other hand will be searchable using normal search engines like Google. This means that ordinary members of the public who haven't already heard of BAILII will be able to find case law with normal searches.

But that's just for starters. There are a whole host of ways we could improve access to UK case law, and we want your ideas too!

What next?

Vast hoards of case law data are still locked away or only released via paid-for services. The battle to open up the UK courts continues!

Why doesn't it have an 'e' in it?

Because at the very start of my very first Trusts seminar our tutor gave us a stern look and said, "You might leave here not knowing very much about Trusts Law, but you will learn how to spell the word 'judgment'. There is no 'e' in it." Apparently, with an 'e' is the American spelling. So there you go.
